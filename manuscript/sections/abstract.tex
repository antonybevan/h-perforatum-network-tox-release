\section*{Abstract}

In network medicine, the proximity Z-score calibrates the interactome distance between a compound's targets and a disease module against a degree-matched null, and its magnitude is compared across compounds to prioritize candidates. We show that such comparisons can obscure two separable quantities: standardized evidence and topological effect size. The null standard deviation of averaged target--disease statistics follows a scaling law, $\sigma_{\text{null}} \propto |T|^{-1/2}$, across shortest-path proximity, random-walk influence, and expression-weighted influence. In a liver-expressed STRING interactome, Hyperforin (10 targets) lies closer to the drug-induced liver injury (DILI) module than Quercetin (62 targets) ($d_c = 1.30$ vs $1.68$, STRING $\geq$900) yet has the weaker proximity Z-score ($-3.86$ vs $-5.44$), and the Z-score ordering, but not the raw-distance ordering, reverses between STRING thresholds. A degree-controlled benchmark using 20{,}000 probes per target-set size locates material reversals within a narrow, high-margin operating regime rather than as a generic outcome. As a complementary effect-size readout, we use perturbation efficiency --- the mean per-target random-walk influence --- and decompose the Hyperforin signal into direct target--DILI overlap (62\% of its efficiency) and a modest propagated advantage over Quercetin ($\sim$1.5$\times$). Prioritizing compounds with unequal target counts therefore requires preserving the distinction between raw effect size, standardized evidence, and direct versus propagated influence.

\noindent\textbf{Keywords:} network medicine; network proximity; proximity Z-score; target-count asymmetry; effect size; random walk with restart; drug-induced liver injury.
